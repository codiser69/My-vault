## Regulating Act, 1773/ East India company act 1773 --
- First time the British Parliament resorted to regulating the affairs of the East India Company.
- The Governor of Bengal was made the Governor-General of Bengal.
- An Executive Council of the Governor-General was created with 4 members.
- It subordinated the Governors of Bombay & Madras to the Governor General of Bengal.
- Supreme Court was established at fort William (Calcutta) as the Apex Court in 1774. Which is also the first High Court.
- It Prohibited officials/servants of company from engaging in private trade and from accepting gifts/bribes from Indians
- This Act led to the establishment of the **Supreme Court in Calcutta** in 1774. The first Chief Justice was **Sir Elijah Impey** & Other three judges -
	a. Lord John Hyde
	b. Lord Robert Chambers 
	c. Stephen Cesar Le Maistre
- Established Board of Directors, consisting of a total of 24 members, was formed to govern the company. This board was required to report its revenue to the British government.
- By this Act, East India company got the rights to trade for 20 years.
## Act of Settlement 1781

- It exempted the Governor-General and the Council from the jurisdiction of the Supreme Court for the acts done by them in their official capacity. Similarly, it also exempted the servants of the company from the jurisdiction of the Supreme Court for their official actions.
- It excluded the revenue matters and the matters arising in the collection of revenue from the jurisdiction of the Supreme Court.
- It provided that the Supreme Court was to have jurisdiction over all the inhabitants of Calcutta. It also required the court to administer the personal law of the defendants i.e., Hindus were to be tried according to the Hindu law and Muslims were to be tried according to the Mohammedan law.
- It laid down that the appeals from the Provincial Courts could be taken to the Governor-General-in-Council and not to the Supreme Court.
- It empowered the Governor-General-in-Council to frame regulations for the Provincial Courts and Councils.

- Commercial and political functions of the company were separated. 
- The Court of Directors managed the commercial function and the Board of Control managed political affairs.
- The company territories in India were called â€˜British possession in Indiaâ€™.
- It Reduced the strength of the Governor General's council to three members.
- Governorâ€™s Councils were set up in Madras and Bombay as well. 
## Charter Act 1813

- It abolished the trade monopoly of the company in India from which the Indian trade was thrown open to all British merchants. However, it continued the monopoly of the company over trade of  tea & trade with China.
- It asserted the sovereignty of the British Crown over the Companyâ€™s territories in India.
- It allowed the Christian missionaries to come to India for the purpose of enlightening the people.
- It provided for the spread of western education among the inhabitants of the British territories in India.

## Charter Act 1833

- It made the Governor-General of Bengal as the Governor-General of India and vested in him all civil and military powers. 
 Thus, **Lord William Bentick** (that time's Governor-General of Bengal) became the first Governor-General of India.
- 1833 Charter act was the most important Act before the Act of 1909.
- This was the first step towards centralization in the British India.
- It deprived the Governor of Bombay and Madras of their legislative powers. The Governor-General of India was given exclusive legislative powers for the entire British India.
- This act ended the commercial activities of the East India company and it was transformed into purely an administrative body.

## Charter Act 1853

- The legislative and executive powers of the Governor-Generalâ€™s Council were separated.
- A Central Legislative Council was created of 6 members out of which 4 were appointed by the provisional governments of Madras, Bombay, Agra and Bengal.
- The Indian civil service was opened as a means to recruit officers for administration through open competition.
- Written examination for ICS was started In 1853.
- It extended the Companyâ€™s rule and allowed it to retain the possession of Indian territories on trust for the British Crown. But it did not specify any particular period, unlike the previous Charters. This was a clear indication that the Companyâ€™s rule could be terminated at any time by the British Parliament.

## Government of India Act 1858

- This Act came After the Revolt of 1857 also known as the first war of Independence or the sepoy mutiny or The Act of Good Government of India, the rule of the company was ended and the British possessions in India came directly under the British Crown. This Act was established the Supremacy of the British crown. 
 English Crown is the example of National executive
- It appointed A secretary of State for India assisted by a 15-member Council of India, having British Cabinet members.
- The Governor-General of India was replaced by the Viceroy of India. Viceroy was the direct representative of the British Crown in India. 
Lord Canning, became the first Viceroy of India & last Governor General of India.
- It ended the system of dual Government by abolishing the Board of Control and Court of Directors.
- It transferred the powers of the Government of India, and the territorial governance in India was subsequently administered.

## Indian Council Act 1909
- This Act also known as Morley-Minto Reforms (Morley was state secretary and Minto was viceroy of India).
- Direct elections to legislative council, first time Attempt at introducing elected Representation in Legislature legitimized  the election of Indian to the various legislative council in India.
- The local bodies were to elect an electoral college, which in turn would elect members of provincial legislatures, who in turn would elect members of the central legislature.
- The elected members were to be indirectly elected
- It considerably increased the size of the legislative councils, both Central and provincial. The number of members in the Central Legislative Council was raised from 16 to 60. The number of members in the provincial legislative councils was not uniform.
- It retained official majority in the Central Legislative Council but allowed the provincial legislative councils to have non-official majority.
- It enlarged the deliberative functions of the legislative councils at both the levels. For example, members were allowed to ask supplementary questions, move resolutions on the budget, and so on.
- Indians for the first time in Viceroy executive council (<u>Satyendra Prasad Sinha</u> as the law member). 
Two Indians were nominated to the Council of the Secretary of State for Indian Affairs.
- Introduced first time a system of communal representation for Muslims by accepting the concept of â€˜separate electorate or constituencies'.

- It also provided for the separate representation of presidency corporations, chambers of commerce, universities and zamindars.
## Government of India Act 1919
- This Act is also known as the Montague-Chelmsford Reforms (Montague was the State secretary and Chelmsford was Viceroy of India )
### Dyarchy 
- Introduction of dyarchy at the provincial level. Dyarchy means a dual set of governments where one set of government was accountable while the other was not.
- Control over provinces was relaxed by demarcating subjects as â€˜central subjectsâ€™ and â€˜provincial subjects
- **Lionel Curtis** is regarded as the **Father of Dyarchy** in the provinces.
### Division of Subjects 
- The provincial government's separated into [[Screenshot_20241125-173356.jpeg|two divisions.]]
	1. Transferred :-
		- the transferred subjects were assigned to the province's Indian ministers.
		- Local self-government, public works, sanitation, industrial research, and the establishment of new companies were all on the Transferred List.
		- The Secretary of State and the Governor-General can intervene, but their intervention is more limited and often only in exceptional circumstances, such as when there are constitutional or legal issues.
		- 
	2. Reserved :-
		- The reserved subjects were under the supervision of the province's British governor, who was not responsible to the legislative council for these matters.
		- Justice Administration, Press, Revenue, Forests, Labour Dispute Settlements, Water, Agricultural Loans, Police, and Prisons were among the items on the Reserved List.
		- The Secretary of State and the Governor-General (or equivalent authorities) have full control and can intervene in these matters as needed.
### Legislative Changes 
- Legislature had no power to pass any bill without the approval  of the Viceroy while on the contrary Viceroy could enact a bill without the legislature assent
- First time Bicameralism was introduced in the Central Legislature by this act. 
- The Lower house was the Legislative Assembly, with 145 members who were either nominated or indirectly elected from the provinces, had tenure of 3-year and the Upper house was the Council of States with 60 members had tenure of 5-year.
- The legislators, under the new reforms, could now ask questions, pass adjournment motions and vote a part of the budget, but 75% of the budget was still not votable.
- The First central Legislative Assembly which was a Legislature for India created by the government of India Act 1919 in 1920.
- The rule made under Indian Act 1919 was known as the Devaluation Rule.
### Electoral provision 
- The communal representation was extended to include Sikhs, Europeans and Anglo-Indians. The franchise (Right of voting) was also granted but only to a limited number of people.
- There was a provision to provide reservation to the non-Brahmins in Madras and the depressed classes were also offered nominated seats in the legislatures.
### Other Provisions 
- The Act provided for the establishment of a Public Service Commission in India.
- The number of Indians in the Executive Council was three out of eight.
- It established an office of the High Commissioner for India in London.

## Government of India Act 1935 
- It is based on Simon Commission report.
- It provided for the establishment of an All-India federation consisting of provinces and princely states as units, though the envisaged federation never comes into being.
- It abolished the Dyarchy in the provinces and introduced â€˜Provincial Autonomyâ€™ in its place, also made provision for the distribution of legislation powers between Centre and the province.
- System of Dyarchy was introduced at the Centre. However, this provision did not come into effect at all.
- The Act divided the powers between the centre and the units in terms of three lists-  
  1. Federal list (59 items), 
  2. provincial list (54 items) and 
  3.  the concurrent list (36 items). Residuary powers were given to the General governor. However, this federation never fructified since princely states did not join it.
- At the faizpur session of the Congress in Dec 1936, Pandit Jwahar lal Nehru described to this Act as "The New Charter of Bondage". 
- The act introduced responsible government in provinces, that is, the governor was required to act with the advice of ministers responsible to the provincial legislature.
- Bicameralism was introduced in six provinces Bengal, Bombay Madras, Bihar, Assam and the United Provinces.
- United Province has the maximum representation in the constituent assembly of India as 31st Dec 1947.
- Separate electorates were further extended to depressed classes, women and labour
- Abolished the Council of India which was established as per the 1858 Act, instead the Secretary of state was  provided with a team of advisors.
- The act provided for setting up(establishment) of - Federal Public Service Commission, Provincial Public Service Commission, Joint Public Service Federal Court, Reserve Bank of India.
- The constitution of India adopt most of institutional framework & procedure details from British constitution and Govt. Act 1935 had the most profound influence in framing the Indian Constitution.
- Election of Provincial legislature and ministries held in 1937.

> [!Cripps Mission(1942)]-
>  The Cripps Mission, led by Sir Stafford Cripps, was sent by the British government to secure India's complete cooperation during World War II.
>  Winston Churchill announced the Cripps Mission on March 11, 1942, and it arrived in India on March 22, 1942. 
>- The British aimed to give India self-governance and 'Dominion Status' after the war, allowing India to control its own affairs and eventually leave the British Commonwealth if it chose. 
 >- A Constituent Assembly would be set up after the IInd world war, with elected representatives from British provinces and princely states. Provinces that disagreed with the constitution could opt to remain separate or independent, supporting the demand for Pakistan.  However, the Muslim League did not agree with this proposal.
>- The Indian Constituent Assembly and the British government would agree on protecting the rights and interests of minorities.
> - During the war, a new Executive Council would be formed, including representatives from different Indian communities, though the defense ministry would remain under British control.
><u>**Rejection of the Mission**:</u>
 >
> - The Indian National Congress rejected the proposal because it sought full independence, not just Dominion Status, and opposed the idea of provinces being able to secede.
> - The Muslim League rejected it because it did not fully satisfy their demand for an independent Pakistan.
> -  The involvement of princely states' nominated representatives in the Constituent Assembly was also controversial.

## Indian Independence Act 1947 
- The British authorities left India on 15th Aug, 1947. It declared India as an Independent & Sovereign state. 
- Divided into the Two Dominion states India & Pakistan and each of them turns sovereign on this very day.
- Lord Mountbatten became the first Governor- General of the new Dominion of India.
- He swore in Jawaharlal Nehru as the first Prime Minister of independent India.
- The powers formerly exercised by the British authorities in India could be transferred to each of those states, and responsible government would be established.
- Punjab & Bengal will be divided and its boundary will be separated by a boundary commission headed by Mr. Radcliffe.
- The Office of the Secretary of State for India will be abrogated.
- Provision was made for the Governor-General for every territory, who was to be named by the Queen of England on the exhortation of the Dominion government. He was not to act in his individual judgment or circumspection however will act just as the constitutional head of the state.
- Each domain must have a sovereign legislature to set the rules. No legislation passed by the British Parliament will automatically apply to India.
- Both countries will have their own Constituent Assembly (constituent & legislative), which will also act as a legislative & Sovereign body.
- Until a constitution is formulated by Constituent Assembly in any dominion, it will work as closely as possible with the 1935 Act.
- Provincial governors will act as constitutional heads of the provinces.
- Designated the viceroy India & Provincial Governors as the constitution (normal heads).
- Reserving the posts of Secretary of State should be discontinued. Government personel wishing to resign after the transfer of power to both dominions must do so.
- British domination of the states and tribal territories of India will end on August 15, 1947. In this case, power will be transferred not to dominions but left to the states to decide whether they want to participate in India or Pakistan.
- From now on, the relationship of the UK government with India will be managed through the Office of Common Wealth Affairs.
- The King of England abandoned the title of King and Emperor of India.
- Pakistani territories include East Bengal, West Pakistan, Sindh and British Baluchistan. Additionally, if the North-West Frontier Province (NWFP) chooses to join Pakistan through a referendum, this territory will also join Pakistan.

> [!Balkan Plan / Dickie Bird Plan / Ismay Plan (1947)]-
>**Lord Mountbatten** developed the 'Dickie Bird Plan' with a committee led by **General Sir Hastings L. Ismay**. It is also known as the **Ismay Plan**.
>The plan proposed transferring power to separate provinces or allowing **Bengal and Punjab** to hold a **plebiscite** for partition. It also allowed princely states to choose whether to join **India**, **Pakistan**, or remain independent.
>**Jawaharlal Nehru** rejected the plan, arguing it would lead to the **Balkanization** of India, causing conflict and violence. Hence, it became known as the **Balkan Plan**
