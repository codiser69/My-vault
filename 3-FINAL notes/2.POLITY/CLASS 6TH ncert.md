# Chapter-3 :: What is Government?
Every country needs a government to make decisions and get things done, The government also has the job of protecting the boundaries of the country and maintaining peaceful relations with other countries. Courts are also part of the government to give punishment to criminals and make decision.

 <u> Why it is necessary for them to do so?</u>
When human beings live and work together, there needs to be some amount of organisation so that decisions can be made.

<u>Types of Government :-</u>
These categories can overlap and vary widely in practice, but they provide a general understanding of the different types of government systems.
1. *Democracy*: A system where the citizens have the power to make decisions through voting. Elected representatives form the government. i.e, USA & India
   in a sense a democracy is rule by the people, where basically they participated in the making of rules. 
2. *Monarchy*: A government led by a king or queen. The monarch may consult a small group but has the final say. It can be absolute (Saudi Arabia) or constitutional (United Kingdom).
3. *Republic*: A form of democracy where the head of state is elected, not a monarch. Examples include France and the United States.
4. ***Oligarchy***: A government where a small group of people have control. Examples include certain historical city-states in Greece.
5. *Authoritarian*: A system where a single leader or a small group has significant control, with limited political freedoms. Examples include North Korea and Belarus.
6. ***Totalitarian***: A more extreme version of authoritarianism, where the government seeks to control almost all aspects of public and private life. Examples include Nazi Germany and the Soviet Union under Stalin.
7. ***Theocracy***: A government based on religious principles, where religious leaders often hold power. Examples include Iran and Vatican City.
8. ***Anarchy***: A situation where there is no government or authority, leading to a state of disorder. This is more theoretical and not often seen as a stable form of government
  
<u>Democratic Governments:-</u>
India is a democracy, established after gaining independence from the British. People worldwide have also struggled to establish democracies. 
Democratic governments in past usually referred as *representative democracies.* In this people choose their representatives through an election process (voting). These representatives make decisions on behalf of the people, keeping in mind their voices and interests. 
These days a government cannot call itself democratic unless it allows universal adult franchise. it means all adults have the rights to vote.

Before independence in India, only a small minority was allowed to vote. Early governments allowed only property-owning, educated men to vote, excluding women, the poor, the property-less, and the uneducated. The country was governed by the rules made by these few men. In the journal Young India (1931), Gandhiji expressed shock at the unfairness of this practice and demanded that all adults have the right to vote known as  <mark style="background: #BBFABBA6;">*Universal Adult Franchise</mark>.*

<u>Why do we have regular elections?</u>
Regular elections limit government power. In India, governments can stay in power for only five years. To continue, they must be re-elected by the people, This is the moment when people can sense their power in a democracy.

 <u>Other ways of participating:- </u>
People participate by taking an interest in government workings and criticizing when necessary. They express their views and influence government actions through dharnas, rallies, strikes, and signature campaigns. Media like newspapers, magazines, and TV also discuss government issues and responsibilities.
Another form of participation is through social movements challenging the government. Minority communities, Dalits, Adivasis, women, and others often engage this way. Observing a rally helps us understand government functioning and the causes people are advocating for.

<u>Need to Resolve Conflict:</u>
Conflicts arise when people from different cultures, religions, regions, or economic backgrounds do not get along or feel discriminated against. Religious events and rivers can also lead to conflicts between states. The government is responsible for resolving these conflicts according to the laws laid down by the Indian Constitution.

<u>EQUALITY AND JUSTICE</u>:-
One key idea of a democratic government is its commitment to Equality and Justice, which are inseparable.
The practice of untouchability is now banned by law. This group was previously denied education, transport, medical facilities, and the chance to offer prayers. Many realized that such practices must not continue and that justice can only be achieved when people are treated equally. i.e, The government promotes justice by offering special provisions, such as waiving or lowering fees for girls in government schools and colleges.

## <mark style="background: #BBFABBA6;">Three Levels of Panchayats :-</mark>
the Panchayati Raj System is a process through which people participate in their own government
1. Panchayati Raj system
2. Block level OR Janpad Panchayat OR the Panchayat Samiti
3. District Panchayat or the Zila Parishad

1.-<u>Panchayati Raj</u>  : -The Panchayati Raj system is the first tier or level of democratic government.
->   Gram Panchayat:
- The Gram Panchayat meets regularly and one of its main tasks is to implement development programmes for all villages that come under it. it needs approval of gram sabha. 
- Divided into wards, each electing a Ward Member (Panch).
- All members of The Gram Sabha elects a Sarpanch (Panchayat President) and forms the Gram Panchayat.
-  The Gram Panchayat is Elected for five years.
- it has a Secretary who is also works for Gram sabha (appointed, not elected) responsible for calling meetings and recording their decisions.
 
**The work of a Gram Panchayat includes**
  1. The construction and maintenance of water sources, roads, drainage, school buildings and other common property resources. 
  2. Levying and collecting local taxes. 
  3. Executing government schemes related to generating employment in the village. 

**Sources of funds for the Panchayat** 
 1. Collection of taxes on houses, market places etc. 
 2. Government scheme funds received through various   departments of the government – through the Janpad and Zila Panchayats. 
 3. Donations for community works etc

-> <u>Gram sabha</u> :- 
- The Gram Sabha is a meeting of all adults who live in the area covered by a Panchayat. Acts as a forum for discussing and making decisions on village matters.This could be only one village or a few villages. 
- Ensures accountability and transparency of the Gram Panchayat.
- Anyone who is 18 years old or more and who has the right to vote is a member of the Gram Sabha.
- The Gram Sabha meeting begins with the Panchayat President  (who is also called the Sarpanch) and the members of the Panchayat (the Panchs).

2.-Panchayat Samiti  :- The Panchayat Samiti has many Gram Panchayats under it

3.-District Panchayat :-Above the Panchayat Samiti  is distt. panchayat. it makes developmental plans at the district level.
